# Ammonia Leak Detection

## Automated detection

The station has two independent paths for detecting ammonia in the cabin:

- **Trace Contaminant Sensors (TCS)** in the US Lab and Node 3 sample cabin
  atmosphere continuously and can identify ammonia specifically. Alarm
  threshold is set well below the IDLH concentration of 300 ppm.
- **Major Constituent Analyzer (MCA)** primarily tracks O₂, CO₂, N₂, H₂,
  methane, and water vapor. It can flag anomalous drops in O₂ partial
  pressure that sometimes accompany large leaks.

When ammonia is detected above threshold, the Caution and Warning (C&W)
system raises an **ATM (Atmosphere)** alarm — the highest-priority audio
and visual annunciation on the station. The ATM alarm takes precedence over
all non-emergency annunciations.

## Manual detection by the crew

Before sensors trip, the crew may notice:

- **Sharp pungent odor** — ammonia's characteristic smell. But ammonia
  deadens the sense of smell rapidly; absence of odor after initial
  detection does not mean the leak has stopped.
- **Burning sensation** in eyes, nose, or throat — an early exposure symptom.
- **Sudden irritation of contact lenses** (crew who wear them).
- **Visible "misting"** near a heat exchanger if the leak is large.
- **Audible hiss** near a bulkhead or heat exchanger.

Any of these warrants an immediate mask-up even without an ATM alarm.

## Distinguishing ammonia from other toxic-atmosphere events

An ATM alarm by itself does not name the contaminant. The crew's first
diagnostic question is whether the alarm is ammonia, fire-byproducts
(CO, HCl, HCN from burning materials), or a coolant leak from the IATCS
water loops (which should not be toxic but can confuse sensors).

Rule of thumb for the crew:

- **Ammonia** — pungent, eye/throat irritation, localized near a heat
  exchanger, no smoke.
- **Fire byproducts** — visible smoke, smoke-detector alarm precedes ATM,
  smell of burning material.
- **IATCS water spill** — visible droplets or mist, no odor, no toxicity,
  but can trigger some sensors.

The correct response to any uncertain toxic-atmosphere alarm is the same
as ammonia: mask up, isolate, then diagnose. The cost of masking up
unnecessarily is low; the cost of not masking up during an ammonia event
is severe lung injury.
