# Fire Emergency

## Detection

Each rack and each module has smoke detectors using scatter
photoelectric technology. Detection is automatic and raises a fire
alarm instantly. Visual confirmation (flame, smoke plume) by crew is
rare but possible.

## Immediate response

1. **Stop ventilation** in the affected module to prevent spreading
   the smoke plume and starving the fire of fresh oxygen-rich air.
   On the US segment, this is done by shutting off the Inter-Module
   Ventilation (IMV) fans.
2. **Don PBE** — fire byproducts (CO, HCl, HCN from burning
   polyurethane, wiring, and other spacecraft materials) are toxic.
3. **Power down** the affected rack or equipment via the C&W panel
   or the local circuit breakers.
4. **Suppress** with CO₂ or water-mist fire extinguishers (PFEs).
   CO₂ for electrical fires, water-mist for ordinary combustibles.
5. **Muster** in an unaffected module with hatches closed.

## Post-fire recovery

Even after suppression, fire byproducts remain in the cabin air.
The crew continues to wear PBE until atmosphere scrubbing confirms
safe levels of CO, HCN, HCl, and other combustion products. The
TCCS and CDRA both contribute to scrubbing.

Equipment that was involved in the fire must be inspected and may
require replacement before repowering.

## Why fire is uniquely dangerous on-orbit

- No convection in microgravity — flames are spherical, smaller,
  but very persistent because fresh oxygen diffuses slowly to the
  surface. A fire that would burn out in seconds on Earth can
  smolder for minutes.
- Smoke doesn't rise; it diffuses in all directions.
- Cabin atmosphere is elevated O₂ partial pressure compared to
  sea-level air, so ignition energy is lower for some materials.
- No fresh air to escape to — once the cabin is contaminated,
  scrubbing is the only recovery.
