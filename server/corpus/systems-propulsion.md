# Propulsion

The ISS has no main propulsion. Routine attitude control is by
CMGs; larger maneuvers use thrusters on the Russian segment or
docked vehicles.

## Thruster groups

- **Zvezda main engines** — two large thrusters on the aft of the
  Service Module. Hydrazine + N₂O₄. Used for orbital reboost.
- **Zvezda attitude thrusters** — smaller thrusters for attitude
  control and smaller maneuvers.
- **Zarya thrusters** — similar to Zvezda's, redundant.
- **Docked Progress thrusters** — each Progress cargo vehicle has
  its own propulsion, which can be commanded to produce torque
  on the station while docked.
- **Docked Cygnus thrusters** — since 2018, Cygnus can provide
  reboost capability.

## Propellants

- **UDMH / MMH** (unsymmetrical dimethylhydrazine / monomethyl
  hydrazine) — fuel. Stored in docked vehicles and Russian
  segment tanks.
- **N₂O₄** (nitrogen tetroxide) — oxidizer. Stored separately.
- **Hydrazine** (N₂H₄) on some attitude thrusters.

All are toxic and corrosive. Propellant handling is controlled;
ground-monitored during every operation.

## Use cases

- **Reboost** — periodic altitude restoration to counter drag.
- **Debris avoidance maneuver (DAM)** — unplanned delta-V to
  avoid a predicted debris conjunction. Typically 1–5 m/s.
- **Attitude desaturation** — help CMGs unload momentum.
- **Docking and undocking assists** — if needed.

## What if propulsion is unavailable?

Short-term (days to weeks): no impact. Atmospheric drag brings
the station down slowly; atmospheric pressure at 400 km is very
low.

Medium-term (months): ISS needs reboost. Without propulsion, the
station would re-enter within 1–2 years.

Long-term (station end-of-life): de-orbit burn requires
significant propulsion; multiple docked vehicles or a dedicated
Deorbit Vehicle provide the delta-V.

## Role in emergency

Propulsion is not directly relevant to ammonia, fire, or depress
events. Exception: MMOD conjunction warnings may trigger a DAM
before an impact event.
