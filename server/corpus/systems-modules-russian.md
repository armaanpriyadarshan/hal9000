# Russian Segment Modules

Built by Roscosmos (formerly RKA). Interfaces the US segment at
Node 1 via a PMA (Pressurized Mating Adapter).

## Modules

### Zarya (FGB)

- First ISS module launched, 1998.
- Originally provided propulsion and power; now primarily stowage
  and transit.
- Pressurized volume: ~72 m³.

### Zvezda (Service Module, SM)

- Second module launched, 2000.
- Life-support hub for Russian segment: Elektron (O₂), Vozdukh
  (CO₂), water recovery.
- Primary crew quarters location on Russian segment.
- Two main engines for reboost.
- Pressurized volume: ~90 m³.

### Poisk (MRM-2)

- Mini-research module, attached to Zvezda zenith.
- Airlock capability for Russian-segment EVAs.
- Soyuz/Progress docking port.

### Rassvet (MRM-1)

- Mini-research module, attached to Zarya nadir.
- Docking port for Soyuz/Progress.

### Nauka (MLM)

- Multipurpose Laboratory Module, installed 2021.
- Russian research module, replaced Pirs.
- European Robotic Arm (ERA) attachment point.
- Pressurized volume: ~70 m³.

### Prichal (Nodal Module)

- Installed 2021 on Nauka nadir.
- Adds multiple docking ports for Soyuz/Progress.

## Life support on Russian segment

- **Elektron** — O₂ generation (water electrolysis).
- **Vozdukh** — CO₂ scrubbing.
- **SKV** — air conditioning / humidity control.
- **SRV-K / SRV-U** — water recovery from condensate / urine.
- **BMP** — harmful impurities filter (equivalent to TCCS).

Russian and US life support run independently but exchange air
via Inter-Module Ventilation (IMV) between Node 1 and Zarya. Each
side can support the full crew in contingency.

## Russian-segment hatch standard

Russian hatches are SSVP (Sistema Stykovki i Vnutrennego
Perekhoda) style on docking ports, different physical standard
from US CBM/IDSS. Internal bulkhead hatches are manual
latching. A PMA adapts between US CBM and Russian SSVP at the
segment boundary.

## Muster implications

During emergencies, the Russian segment is often a viable muster
location if the leak is on the US side. Zvezda and Zarya combined
provide ~160 m³, life support, and ready access to Soyuz lifeboats.
