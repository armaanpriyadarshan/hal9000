# Ammonia Leak Overview

Ammonia (NH₃) is the working fluid in the International Space Station's
External Active Thermal Control System (EATCS). The two external ammonia
coolant loops (Loop A and Loop B) reject the station's waste heat to space
through two sets of radiators. Combined, they circulate several hundred
kilograms of anhydrous liquid ammonia at high pressure.

The Internal Active Thermal Control System (IATCS) loops that run through
pressurized modules use water, not ammonia, as their coolant. The two
systems exchange heat through the Heat Exchangers (IFHX and CHX) that are
the only places pressurized ammonia physically touches the inside of the
station's structure.

## Why an ammonia leak matters

A leak of external ammonia into the cabin is one of the three most dangerous
non-emergency-abort scenarios the crew trains for. Ammonia is:

- **Toxic.** Immediately Dangerous to Life or Health (IDLH) at 300 ppm for
  OSHA, lethal at ~5000 ppm with short exposure. Causes severe eye, throat,
  and lung injury at a small fraction of those levels.
- **Persistent in a sealed habitat.** A small leak into ~900 cubic meters of
  cabin atmosphere grows to dangerous concentrations in minutes, not hours.
- **Hard to vent.** The crew cannot simply open a window; they have to scrub
  the cabin air through filters, which is slow.
- **Difficult to localize.** Anhydrous ammonia is nearly odorless at low
  concentrations — by the time crew smells it, they have already been
  exposed above IDLH.

## Where ammonia can enter the cabin

Only through a failure of one of the Heat Exchangers: IFHX in the US segment
or CHX in the Russian segment. A pinhole or rupture in the heat-exchanger
wall allows high-pressure external ammonia to cross into the low-pressure
internal water loop, and from there into the cabin through a secondary leak
or outgassing.

## Response priorities, in order

1. **Protect the crew airway.** Portable Breathing Equipment (PBE) on
   *before* doing anything else. Even seconds of unprotected breathing at
   IDLH concentrations injures lungs.
2. **Isolate the source segment.** Close hatches between modules to prevent
   ammonia spread while the leak is characterized.
3. **Scrub the cabin air.** Route atmosphere through carbon filters and
   the CDRA CO₂ scrubbers, which also adsorb ammonia.
4. **Identify and isolate the leak path.** Close the IATCS pump that's
   feeding the leaking heat exchanger; cross-feed cooling from the other
   loop if possible.
5. **Abandon-ship decision.** If the leak cannot be isolated and cabin
   concentrations keep rising, evacuate to Soyuz or Dragon as the safe
   haven.
